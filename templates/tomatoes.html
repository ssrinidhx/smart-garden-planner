Tomatoes

Solanum lycopersicum

Category: Nightshades

Tomatoes are a favorite garden vegetable. (Yes, we technically eat the fruit of the tomato plant, but it's used as a vegetable in eating and cooking and, thus, usually categorized in vegetables.) This vine plant is fairly easy to grow and will produce a bumper crop with proper care. Its uses are versatile, however, tomatoes are susceptible to a range of pests and diseases.

Growing From Seed

Tomatoes are best started indoors in flats. When the first true leaves develop, it is time to transplant.

Planting Considerations

Tomatoes can be either determinate(bush) or indeterminate(climbing) varieties. Determinate varieties can be grown without support, can be planted about 2 feet apart, and they typically yield all of their fruit in the span of a few weeks. Indeterminate varieties should be staked, trellised, or caged, and pruned for optimal yields, and should be planted around 3 feet apart. They yield their fruit over a more extended time period. Tomatoes make great neighbors for basil plants in the garden.

Feeding

Water seedlings with a high-phosphate fertilizer solution for best results.

Harvest

Leave your tomatoes on the vine as long as possible. The perfect tomato for picking will be firm and very red in color, regardless of size, with perhaps some yellow remaining around the stem. A ripe tomato will be only slightly soft. Pick at the base of the stem.

Storage

Never refrigerate whole fresh tomatoes. Doing so spoils the flavor and texture that make up that garden tomato taste. Store whole tomatoes at room temperature, stem side down to reduce bruising. Cut tomatoes should be stored in the fridge to prevent bacterial growth, with the cut side wrapped tightly in plastic wrap.
