Carrot

Daucus carota subsp. sativus

Category: Roots

Carrots are a popular root vegetable that is easy to grow in loose, sandy soil. They are resistant to most pests and diseases, and are a good late season crop that can tolerate frost. Not all carrots are orange; varieties vary in color from purple to white, and colorful seed blends are easily found at most garden centers!

Growing From Seed

Carrots germinate best at 55-65 degrees Fahrenheit; high summer temperatures will result in lower quality growth.

Planting Considerations

Carrots are biennial plants. If you leave them in the ground, the tops will flower and produce seeds the second year. They have difficulty growing in heavy, compact soil. Radishes are a very good companion plant to carrots!

Feeding

Fertilize around 5-6 weeks after sowing, but don't feed with high nitrogen fertilizer. Excess nitrogren promotes foliage growth, and we're looking for root growth here!

Harvest

Carrots are mature at around 2 ½ months and ½ inch in diameter. You may harvest whenever desired maturity is reached; generally, smaller carrots taste better. You can harvest in stages, as roots reach full size, to stagger your harvest over a longer period of time. Make sure to harvest before it gets too warm, or else your roots will get fibrous. Frosts will cause these plants to store sugar in the roots, so carrots will be sweeter after a frost! Carrots can also be container grown; harvest these when they've reached finger size.

Storage

To store freshly harvested carrots, twist off the tops, scrub off the dirt under cold running water, let dry and seal in airtight plastic bags, and refrigerate. This prevents the fresh carrots from going limp quickly in the fridge. You may leave mature carrots in the soil for storage; frosts result in sweeter carrots!.
